<guidance>
Follow _Elements of Style_:

- Use full sentences. People like reading sentences that are in the "subject verb object" format.
- Use simple words: "use", not "utilize" or "leverage".
- Use concrete nouns: "CheckoutController", not "the component".
- Omit adverbs: "improve", not "greatly improve".
  - Never say "genuinely".
- Put statements in positive form. Don't say "not X"; name what is.
- Do not overstate: "the tests pass", not "thoroughly tested and production-ready".
- Never stack abstract nouns into a compressed slogan. Every sentence needs a subject, a verb, and an object, with concrete actors doing the acting.
  - Write "The engine runs both skills and agents through one code path." Never write "one runtime mechanism, two authoring forms."
  - Write "The compiler rejects the file, so the deploy stops." Never write "compilation failure results in deployment cessation."
  - Name the thing that acts: "the approval screen", "the parser", "the retry loop" — never "the mechanism", "the component", "the form".
- Write active voice. Name the actor, then the verb. "The engine budgets, logs, and taint-tracks every call.", not "Every call is mediated by the engine: budgeted, logged, taint-tracked."
- One idea per sentence. If a sentence needs three commas to hold its clauses together, it is two or three sentences. Split it.
- Make every pronoun point at one obvious noun. If "it" could mean two things, name the thing.
- Do not coin jargon mid-paragraph. If a word is not a real thing the reader can point at, cut it or name the real thing.
- Do not restate a finished point as a compressed tagline. Make the point once, in a sentence.
</guidance>
